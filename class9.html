    Q 1: Write a note on Abacus, Napier’s Bones, Pascaline, Difference Engine, Analytical Engine and Mark-I?
    Abacus:
    ○ Abacus is one of the oldest calculating devices.
    ○ It consists of a frame with rods and movable beads.
    ○ It is used to perform addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.
    ○ The abacus helps in fast mental calculations and improves number skills.
    ○ It played an important role in the early history of mathematics and computing.
    Napier’s Bones:
    ○ Napier’s Bones were invented by John Napier in 1617.
    ○ They are a set of numbered rods made of bone, wood, or metal.
    ○ The device was used for multiplication, division, and square roots.
    ○ It worked by changing calculations into simple addition and subtraction.
    ○ Napier’s Bones were an important early calculating tool before modern calculators.
    Pascaline:
    ○ Pascaline was an early mechanical calculating machine.
    ○ It was invented by Blaise Pascal in 1642.
    ○ It was mainly used for addition and subtraction.
    ○ The machine worked using gears and wheels.
    ○ Pascaline helped reduce calculation errors and led to modern calculators.
    Difference Engine:
    ○ Difference Engine was an early mechanical computing machine.
    ○ It was designed by Charles Babbage in the 1820s.
    ○ It was used to calculate mathematical tables automatically.
    ○ The machine worked using the method of differences to avoid errors.
    ○ The Difference Engine is considered an important invention in the history of computers.
    Analytical Engine:
    ○ Analytical Engine was a proposed general-purpose mechanical computer.
    ○ It was designed by Charles Babbage in 1837.
    ○ It could perform complex calculations automatically.
    ○ The machine had parts similar to a modern computer: input, processing, memory, and output.
    ○ The Analytical Engine is considered the foundation of modern computer concepts.
    Mark-I:
    ○ The next successful computing machine invented was a digital computer known as Mark-I.
    ○ It was invented by Howard Aiken in 1944.
    Uses:
    ○ Mark-I could add three numbers having eight digits in one second.
    ○ It could print out its result on punched cards or on an electric typewriter.
    Structure:
    ○ Mark-I was 50 feet long, 8 feet high and weighed about 5 tons.
    ○ It used 3000 electric switches.
    Model:
    ○ Mark-II, Mark-III and Mark-IV.
    Q 2: Describe the evolution of computer generations from the first generation to the fourth generation, highlighting the key technological developments and their impact on computing
    Computer Generations:
    Computer generation means a stage in the development of computers, where each stage is marked by a major improvement in technology that makes computers faster, smaller, and more efficient.

    First Generation Computers (1940–1956)
    Technology Used / Development:
    ○ Vacuum tubes were used in the first-generation computers.
    ○ Input was based on punched cards.
    ○ Output was obtained on printouts through electric typewriter.
    Features / Impact:
    ○ Speed was slow and memory was very small.
    ○ They were huge in size taking up entire room.
    ○ They were very expensive and unreliable.
    ○ They consume a lot of power and generate a lot of heat.
    ○ Machine language was used in these computers.
    Model:
    ○ ENIAC, UNIVAC 1, IBM 604, Mark-I etc.

    Second Generation Computers (1956–1963)
    Technology Used:
    Transistors were used in the second-generation computers, developed by William Shockley, John Bardeen, and Walter Brattain in 1947.
    Features / Characteristics:
    ○ Transistors reduced the size of computers and increased the speed and memory capacity.
    ○ Input was based on punched card readers, magnetic tapes, and magnetic disks.
    ○ Output was obtained on printers.
    Model:
    ○ UNIVAC II, IBM 7030, NCR Series etc.


    Third Generation Computers (1963–1971)
    Technology Used:
    ICs (Integrated Circuits) were used in the third-generation computers, along with transistors, diodes, and resistors.
    Features / Impacts:
    ○ These computers were faster and had improved memory.
    ○ They were small in size.
    ○ They were cheaper and more reliable.
    ○ They consumed less power and generated less heat.
    ○ Input was based on keyboard.
    ○ Output was obtained on monitor.
    ○ These computers used more versatile programs like real-time programming.
    ○ These computers could run different application programs at the same time.
    Model:
    ○ Burroughs 6700, IBM System/360, System 3, and Control Corporation’s 3300 etc.


    Fourth Generation Computers (1971–Present)
    Technology Used:
    ○ LSI (Large Scale Integration) and VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration) chips having millions of transistors were developed.
    ○ Microprocessor was also used in this generation.
    Features / Impacts:
    ○ They are very fast and have large storage capacity.
    ○ They are small in size, very reliable, consume less power, and are affordable.
    ○ Large variety of software is available for use in this generation.
    ○ Advanced input/output devices are used.
    ○ Operating System with Graphical User Interface (GUI) was developed that supports multimedia.
    ○ They support a wide range of portable and wireless input/output devices.
    ○ These computers support modern languages such as Visual Basic, C++, Java etc.
    Model:
    ○ Pentium Series, Dual Core, Core2 Duo, Core i3, i5, i7 etc.

    Potential Challenges / Objectives:
    ○ The objective of fifth generation is to develop devices that can understand and respond to natural languages, developing systems capable of NLP (Natural Language Processing) and have thinking power.
    ○ This generation of computers is based on Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies and aims to develop machines (called robots) with the capability to learn, think, reason, and solve problems automatically and independently.
    Features / Impacts:
    ○ AI will minimize the need for typed programs.
    ○ The key objective is to develop advanced Expert Systems (ES), which are software that have decision-making abilities like a human expert in specific fields such as medical, mining, and engineering.
    ○ These computers will allow users to give commands in any natural language such as English using voice recognition.

  • 1.4 Von Neumann Architecture
    Q 5: Describe Von Neumann Architecture?
    Characteristics of Von Neumann-based Computer
    ○ Uses a single processor.
    ○ Uses one memory for both instructions and data.
    ○ Executes programs following the fetch-decode-execute cycle.
    Components of Von-Neumann Model
    1. Central Processing Unit
    2. Buses
    3. Memory Unit
    4. Input / Output (I/O) Controller
    1. Central Processing Unit (CPU)
    ○ A microprocessor is the main chip on the motherboard that controls all the activities of the computer.
    ○ It is known as Central Processing Unit (CPU) or simply processor.
    ○ It has three parts i.e. Control Unit (CU), Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU) and registers.
    a. Control Unit (CU)
    ○ The Control Unit of a computer system controls the operations of components like ALU, memory and input/output devices.
    ○ The Control Unit consists of a program counter that contains the address of the instructions to be fetched and an instruction register into which instructions are fetched from memory for execution.
    b. Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU)
    ○ ALU is the part of the Central Processing Unit (CPU) that performs all the arithmetic and logical operations.
    ○ It consists of two parts.
    ○ i. Arithmetic Unit: It performs all the arithmetic operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication and division.
    ○ ii. Logic Unit: It performs logical operations which include comparisons of numbers or alphabets.
    c. Registers
    ○ Registers are small, high-speed storage units located within the CPU.
    ○ They hold data that is frequently used by the CPU during processing.
    ○ Data is quickly transferred between registers and main memory via the buses.
    Register Full Name Function
    IMR Memory Address Register Holds the memory location of data that needs to be accessed
    MDR Memory Data Register Holds data that is being transferred to or from memory
    AC Accumulator Stores intermediate arithmetic and logic results
    PC Program Counter Contains the memory address of the next instruction to be executed
    CIR Current Instruction Register Contains the current instruction during processing
    2. Buses
    ○ Buses are the pathways or lines by which data is transmitted from one part of a computer to another, connecting all major internal components to the CPU and memory.
    ○ A standard CPU system bus is comprised of a control bus, data bus and address bus.
    Types of Buses
    Von-Neumann Architecture is comprised of three major bus systems for data transfer.
    Bus Type Function
    Address Bus Carries the addresses of data (but not the data) between the processor and memory
    Data Bus Carries data between the processor, the memory unit and the input/output devices
    Control Bus Carries control signals or commands from the CPU in order to control and coordinate all the activities within the computer

    3. Memory Unit
    ○ In the Von Neumann architecture, the three types of memory commonly mentioned are:

    1. Primary Memory (Main Memory or RAM):
    ○ This is the memory directly accessible by the CPU for storing and retrieving data that is actively being used or processed.
    ○ It is volatile memory, meaning it loses its contents when the power is turned off.

    2. Cache Memory:
    ○ This is a smaller, faster type of volatile memory located closer to the CPU.
    ○ It is used to temporarily hold frequently accessed data and instructions, improving the speed and efficiency of data retrieval.

    3. Secondary Memory (Storage):
    ○ This refers to non-volatile memory used for long-term storage of data and programs.
    ○ Examples include hard drives, solid-state drives, and other storage devices.
    ○ Unlike primary memory, secondary memory retains data even when the power is off.

    ○ These three types of memory work together to provide the necessary storage and retrieval functions required by the Von Neumann architecture for executing programs and processing data.
    4. Input / Output (I/O) Controller
    This component manages the flow of data between the CPU and external devices such as hard drives, USB devices, and network interfaces.

    Q 6. Define Data Transmission and its basic terms within a Computer System?
    Data Transmission:
    Data Transmission means sending data (information) from one place to another.
    In a computer system, this can be between:
    • Two computers
    • A computer and a printer
    • A computer and the internet
    The data can be in the form of text, audio, video, or images.

    Basic Terms in Data Transmission:
    Bus Architecture:
    Bus Architecture is the way data moves inside a computer.
    It refers to a system of wires or pathways (called "buses") that connect the different parts of a computer—like the CPU, memory, and input/output devices—so they can communicate with each other.

    Data Paths:
    Data Paths are the routes or paths inside a computer where data moves between different parts like the CPU, memory, and input/output devices.
    They include the buses, registers, and circuits that help in carrying and processing data.

    Instruction Cycle:
    The Instruction Cycle is the process a computer follows to fetch, understand, and carry out one instruction from a program.
    It’s like the computer’s way of thinking and acting step by step.

    Interrupts:
    Interrupts are signals that tell the CPU to pause what it’s doing and handle something important right away.
    It's like someone tapping you on the shoulder while you're working — you stop for a moment to see what they need.

    Parallelism:
    Parallelism in computers means doing many tasks at the same time instead of one after another.
    It helps a computer work faster and be more efficient by splitting work into smaller parts that run together.

    Simple Example:
    Imagine you are folding clothes and your friend is washing dishes.
    Both jobs are being done at the same time — that’s parallelism!

    1. Built-Up or Manufactured Memory:
    Memory Built-in means, memory chips that are physically integrated into the main circuit board, also known as the motherboard.
    Examples / Types: Chip memory, Magnetic memory and Optical memory.

    2. Chip Memory (How Chip Memory is better than other traditional forms of memories):
    Chip is a small piece of semi-conducting material (usually silicon).
    A small circuit called IC (Integrated Circuit) is embedded on it.
    A typical chip contains millions of electronic components (transistors).

    How Chip Memory is better than other traditional forms of memories:
    Chip memories are better in speed as compared to other memories due to the following reasons:
    • Chip memories do not have mechanical moving parts.
    • Unlike traditional forms of memory, chip memories rely on electric currents for their operation.
    • This reliance on electrical processes contributes to rapid data access and retrieval.

    Examples of Chip Memory:
    RAM, ROM and Cache memory
    Flash memory drives
    Memory cards
    Registers
    Solid State Drives (SSDs)

    3. Magnetic Memory (What are Magnetic Memories? How it works? Advantages and Disadvantages):
    Magnetic memory is a type of memory that uses magnetic materials to store data.
    This technology is based on the magnetic properties of materials to store binary information (0s and 1s).

    Types of Magnetic Memory:
    Hard Disk Drives (HDDs)
    Magnetic Tape

    Advantages of Magnetic Memory:
    Non-Volatility: Retains data even when power is turned off.
    High Capacity: Can store large amounts of data.
    Cost-Effective: Generally cheaper storage.

    Disadvantages of Magnetic Memory:
    Speed: Slower access speeds compared to solid-state drives (SSDs).
    Mechanical Parts: HDDs have moving parts that may fail mechanically.

    Modern Use of Magnetic Memory:
    While SSDs and newer technologies are becoming more popular due to speed and durability,
    magnetic memory is still important for large-scale data storage, backups, and archival purposes.

    4. Optical Memory:
    Optical memory is a type of memory that uses light, typically a laser, to read and write data.

    Examples of Optical Memory:
    CDs
    DVDs
    Blu-ray Discs (BDs)

    Advantages of Optical Memory:
    Portability: Optical discs are compact and easy to transport.
    Longevity: Can store data for many years if properly handled.
    Cost-Effective: Generally cheaper storage.

    Disadvantages of Optical Memory:
    Speed: Slower read/write speeds compared to hard drives and SSDs.
    Capacity: Lower storage capacity compared to modern SSDs and HDDs.
    Physical Damage: Can be damaged by scratches and cracks.

    Retention Power:
    The ability of a system to retain data even when the power is turned off is called retention power.

    Types of Retention Power:
    Volatile Memory
    Non-Volatile Memory

    Q 10 (i): Explain Common Types of System software?
    There are following common types of System Software:

    An operating system is system software that manages computer hardware and software resources, and provides common services for computer programs.
    It is also link between hardware and application software.
    Example: Microsoft Windows, macOS, Linux, and Android

    ii. Device Drivers:
    • A device driver is system software that controls the operation of a computer device.
    • When users attach a device such as printer or scanner to their computer, they should install its driver to make it operational.
    Device drivers are provided by device manufactures.

    iii. Utility Programs:
    Utility programs perform specific tasks that are related to the management of the computer.

    • Linkers and loaders are programs that help with the execution of programs.
    • Linkers combine multiple object files (compiled code) into a single executable file, while loaders load these files into memory for execution.

    • Firmware is a type of software that is permanently stored on hardware devices.
    • It provides low-level control over the device's operation.
    Examples: BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) in a computers motherboard or the firmware in a digital washing machine.

    Q 20: Discuss the advantages and limitations of different network topologies, including bus, star, ring, and mesh. When should each topology be used in a network design?
    Network Topology:
    Network topologies define the arrangement of various elements (links, nodes, etc.) in a computer network.
    A node represents a computer or a network device.
    Types of Network Topologies:
    Commonly used topologies are bus, ring, star and mesh.
    1. Bus Topology:
    It is the simplest network topology.
    It consists of a single central cable known as bus.
    All the devices are connected to the bus along its length to communicate with each other.
    At each end of bus, a device called terminator is attached so that the signals do not bounce back on the bus causing errors.
    Features/Advantages of Bus Topology:
    • Suitable for small networks.
    • Easy to add new computers.
    • Requires less cable to implement.
    Limitations of Bus Topology:
    • If the single cable is damaged at any point, the entire network stops working.
    • Difficult to detect and fix faults.
    • Not suitable for large network.
    When to Use:
    Small, temporary networks or environments with a limited number of devices, such as a home or small office network.
    2. Star Topology:
    In star topology, all the nodes are connected to central device called switch or hub.
    It is one of the commonly used network topologies.
    A switch can connect 4, 8, 16, 24 or 32 nodes.
    A switch can be connected to another switch to expand the network.
    Features/Advantages of Star Topology:
    • Easy to connect new device to the network.
    • Easy to detect and fix faults.
    • Failure of one computer does not stop functioning of the entire network.
    Limitations of Star Topology:
    • Lengthy cable is required for connecting two computers.
    • Costly to implement.
    When to Use:
    It is suitable for both small and large networks, such as in corporate offices or data centers.
    3. Ring Topology:
    The ring network topology is shaped just like a ring.
    It is like a bus with both ends connected together.
    All the messages travel in the same direction.
    Message from one node is sent to the next node.
    Message is received by the intended node, otherwise it is ignored and passed on until the destination is reached.
    Features/Advantages of Ring Topology:
    • Each node is directly connected to the ring.
    • Easy to install and wire.
    • Data on the network flows in one direction.
    • Not costly to implement.
    Limitations of Ring Topology:
    • If ring is broken at any point, the entire network stops functioning.
    • Detection of fault is difficult.
    • If any computer in the ring is not working, the whole network is affected.
    When to Use:
    Situations requiring equal access and predictable performance, such as in token ring networks and some metropolitan area networks (MANs).
    4. Mesh Topology:
    In Mesh network topology, all the network nodes are directly connected to all other nodes.
    Message sent on a mesh network can take any possible path from source to destination.
    It is not commonly used since it is costly and difficult to implement.
    Features/Advantages of Mesh Topology:
    • It is the most reliable network topology.
    • Alternative paths are available in case a path is broken from source to destination.
    • Can manage high traffic.
    Limitations of Mesh Topology:
    • Most expensive topology to implement.
    • Difficult to implement as compared to other topologies.
    • Difficult to add new computer.
    When to Use:
    Best for environments where reliability and fault tolerance are critical, such as large-scale and critical infrastructure networks.

    Q 22: What is the OSI model, and how does it help in understanding the process of data communication? Explain each of the seven layers and their functions.?
    OSI (Open Systems Interconnection) Model:
    The OSI Model (Open Systems Interconnection Model) is like a step-by-step guide that explains how data moves through a computer network in 7 clear steps or layers.
    It helps different devices and systems communicate with each other, even if they are made by different manufacturers.

    How It Helps:
    The OSI model is like a map for data communication — it shows the path, explains each stop, and helps to fix issues when things go wrong.

    The Seven Layers of OSI Model:
    The seven layers of OSI model are described below:

    Layer 7 – Application Layer
    Application Layer provides services to end-user.
    It interacts with the operating system or application software whenever the user wants to send files, read messages or perform other network related activities.

    Layer 6 – Presentation Layer
    Presentation Layer takes the data provided by the Application Layer and converts it into a standard format that the other layers can understand.
    At the receiving end it also formats the information so that it looks the way the user can understand.

    Layer 5 – Session Layer
    Session Layer performs functions that enable two applications or two pieces of the same application to communicate across the network.
    It performs security, name recognition, logging and other similar functions.
    It also establishes, maintains and ends communication with the receiving computer.

    Layer 4 – Transport Layer
    Transport Layer establishes connections between two computers on the network.
    It handles quality control by making sure that the data received is in the right format and the right order.

    Layer 3 – Network Layer
    Network Layer decides which physical path-way the data should take to reach the destination.
    The communication device works in network layer.

    Layer 2 – Data Link Layer
    Data Link Layer defines the format of data on the network.
    This layer converts the data into packets and checks them before putting them on the path-way.
    The communication device Switch works in this layer.

    Layer 1 – Physical Layer
    Physical Layer defines cables and signaling.
    It provides hardware means such as cables and connectors for sending and receiving data.
    Cables, hubs and repeaters work in this layer.

    Practical Example: Sending an Email
    Consider sending an email:
    1. Application Layer: The email client application uses SMTP to send the email.
    2. Presentation Layer: The email content is encoded and possibly encrypted.
    3. Session Layer: A session is established between the sender’s email client and the mail server.
    4. Transport Layer: The email data is broken into segments, and TCP ensures reliable delivery.
    5. Network Layer: IP addresses are used to route the email through various networks to the recipient’s mail server.
    6. Data Link Layer: Frames are created for data transfer over the physical network.
    7. Physical Layer: The raw bits are transmitted over physical media such as Ethernet cables or Wi-Fi.

    Designers test the prototype, collect feedback, improve the solution, and make changes if needed.

    Sustainable Development:
    Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

    • Instead of just cutting down trees for wood, plant new trees also.
    • Instead of using traditional energy resources, use renewable energy sources like solar and wind power.
    • Promoting public transportation and eco-friendly urban planning.

    1. No Poverty
    2. Zero Hunger
    3. Good Health and Well-being
    4. Quality Education
    5. Gender Equality
    6. Clean Water and Sanitation
    7. Affordable and Clean Energy

    Q19. What is TAM (Total Addressable Market)? Explain the top-down approach, bottom-up approach, and value theory approach with formulas and examples.
    Calculation of TAM (Total Addressable Market):
    TAM is a crucial concept in business and marketing that estimates the overall revenue opportunity available for a product or service.
    There are three ways to calculate how big your business market can be:
    1. Top-Down Approach:
    This approach uses existing market reports, industry data, and research to estimate the market size.
    Formula: TAM = Total Market Size × Target Segment Percentage
    Example:
    Total smartphone market size = $1 million
    Target segment Percentage for gaming smartphones = 30%
    TAM = 1,000,000 × 30% = 300,000
    2. Bottom-Up Approach:
    This method calculates TAM using internal company data, such as sales, pricing, and customer base.
    Formula: TAM = Total Number of Potential Customers × Price per Product
    Example:
    Total Number of Potential Customers = 10,000
    Price per product = $200
    TAM = 10,000 × 200 = $2,000,000
    3. Value Theory Approach:
    This is based on how much value your product delivers to customers.
    Formula: TAM = Total Number of Potential Customers × Value Created Per Customer
    Example:
    Total Number of Potential Customers = 5,000
    Value Created Per Customer = $1,000/year
    TAM = 5,000 × 1,000 = 5,000,000
    Q20. What does SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) represent, how is it different from TAM, and include the formula with an example.
    SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market or Serviceable Available Market):
    It represents the portion of the Total Addressable Market (TAM) that your business can realistically target, based on factors like geography, product/service offerings, and market reach.
    Formula: SAM = TAM × Relevant Market Segment Percentage
    Example:
    TAM: The global smartphone market is $500 billion.
    Relevant Market Segment Percentage: Your Company operates only in North America, where the market is $150 billion.
    SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):
    It represents the portion of the Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) that your business can realistically capture within a specific time frame, considering your current resources, competition, and market positioning.
    Formula: SOM = SAM × Market Share Percentage
    Example:
    TAM: Global smartphone market = $500 billion.
    SAM: North American smartphone market = $150 billion.
    SOM: If your company can realistically capture 5% of the North American market:
    SOM = 150 billion × 5% = 7.5 billion
    Your SOM is $7.5 billion.
